## what is the stock market?
the stock market is a marketplace. people buy and sell ownership stakes in companies and other financial assets. when a company wants to raise money, it can sell pieces of itself to the public. these pieces are called *shares* or *stocks*. investors buy these pieces hoping the company can grow in value so their stake becomes worth more.

the market itself is a network of exchanges and digital platforms where these trades happen.

## what can you trade?
- stocks: partial owndership in a company.
- bonds: loans you give to a company or government. they pay you back with interest over time. lower risk but lower reward than stocks.
- ETFs (exchange traded funds): a basket of many stocks bundled into one tradeable unit. buying one ETF might give you exposure to 500 companies at once.
- mutual funds: similar to ETFs but managed by a professional, and priced once per day (not traded live like stocks)
- options: contract that give you the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell a stock at a specific price by a certain date. more complex, higher risk.
- futures: agreements to buy or sell something at a set price on a future date. common with commodities like oil or wheat.
- commodities: raw materials like gold, oil, corn.
- cryptocurrencies: digital assets. not on traditional stock exchanges.
- REITs (real estate investment trusts): companies that own real estate, traded liek stocks. 

## common terms
- bull, bull market, bullish: like how a bull fights, aiming its horns upwards, prices are rising and there is general optimism. everyone is buying.
- bear, bear market, bearish: like how a bear fights, swiping downwards, prices are falling 20%+ from recent highs and there is general pessimism.
- portfolio: the total collection of all your investments.
- diversification: spreading your money across different assets so one bad investment doesn't wipe you out entirely.
- liquidity: how easily you can convert an asset to cash. stocks are very liquid as they can sell in seconds, whereas houses are not.
- volatility: how much an asset's price swings up and down. high volatility = more risk, more potential reward.
- dividend: a portion of the company's profits paid out to shareholders, usually quarterly. passive income from owning stock.
- market cap: the total value of a company's stock. calculated as the share price * total shared outstanding. a rough measure of company size.
- index: a benchmark that tracks a group of stocks. the S&P 500 tracks the 500 largest US companies. the Dow Jones tracks 30 major ones.
- broker: the intermediary you use to place trades (Fidelity, Schwab, Robinhood)
- ask vs bid: *ask* is what the sellers want for a stock; the *bid* is what buyers are willing to pay. the gap between them is the spread.
- order types:
    - market order: buy/sell immediately at the current price
    - limit order: only buy/sell if a price hits a level you specify
    - stop-loss: automatically sells if price drops to a set point, to cap your losses.

## more specific terms
- brokerage firms: a licensed company that acts as the middleman between you and the market.
    - they make money through commissions (a fee per trade), spreads (the difference between buy/sell prices), interest on cash or margin accounts
    - two main flavors:
        - full-service brokers who assign you an advisor who gives your personalized recommendations (Morgan Stanley, Merrill Lynch)
        - discount/self-directed brokers where you make your own decisions, they execute (Fidelity, Schwab, Robinhood)
- financial firms: an umbrella term including brokerages and others:
    - investment banks: help companies raise money, go public (IPO), or merge (Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan).
        - don't take deposits.
        - trade securities and advise on big deals
    - commercial banks: your everyday bank (Chase, Bank of America)
    - asset management firms: manage large pools of money for clients (BlackRock, Vanguard)
        - make money on behalf of others. pension funds, endowments, wealthy individuals.
        - decide where the invest pooled capital.
    - insurance companies: also invest premiums
    - hedge funds: private investment funds that use aggressive, flexible strategies (Bridgewater, Two Sigma, Citadel (the fund))
        - only accessible to very wealthy investors or institutional investors.
        - use strategies like shorting stocks, leverage, derivatives, and global macro bets. 
    - private equity: buy entire companies or large stakes, restructure/improve them, then sell for profit. long time horizons (5-10 years). (KKR, Blackstone, Apollo)
    - quant trading firms: find and exploit pricing inefficiencies in markets, trading their own money, not clients'. (Jane Street, Citadel Securities, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Virtu)
    - the main distinction is that brokerage firms execute trades, but financial firms are a broader label covering anyone in the business of managing, moving, or growing money.
- electronic brokerage firms operate electronic communications networks (ECNs) which are fully automated platforms that match buyers and sellers directly. emerged to make trading faster, cheaper, and more transparent.
    - trades happen in fractions of a seconds
    - offer trading outside normal market hours
    - reduced spreads because they cut out intermediaries
    - how most modern trading works
- holdings: everything you own in your investment accounts
- market making: 
    - imagine you want to sell your car, but there is no buyer right now. a market maker is essentially a dealership that says "we'll buy it from you right now" and then turns around and sells it to the next buyer who comes along. they're always ready to be on either side of a trade.
        - they profit on the spread of each transaction
    - why does the market need market makers? if you wanted to sell 1000 shares of a stock, you'd have to wait for someone who specifically wanted to buy 1000 shares at that momeny. they provide liquidity and are always available. 
- arbitrage: profiting from the same thing being priced differently in two places at the same time
    - if a sneaker sells for $100 in one store but $150 in another, and you buy from the cheap store and sell to the expensive buyer, the gap is your profit -- no opinion, no bet, just exploiting a price discrepancy.
- security: any tradeable financial asset that represents value or an ownership/debt claim.
    - umbrella term for everything traded in financial markets
    - three main buckets:
        - equity securities: stocks. you own a piece of a company
        - debt securities: bonds. you're owed money back with interest
        - derivative securities: options, futures. their value is derived from an underlying asset.
    - groups of securities: a collection of multiple securities bundled or analyzed together
- Coinbase: the Fidelity or Schwab of crypto, it is a cryptocurrency exchange and brokerage.
- short positions
- blockchain: a digital ledger, a shared spreadsheet that records transactions. 
    - it's decentralized (not owned by a single company or government) so thousands of computers around the world all hold identical companies
    - it's immutable (once something is written on it, it can't be erased or altered)
    - it's transparent (anyone can view the record)
- token on blockchain: an entry on the ledger that represents ownership of something. can be almost anything
    - currency (bitcoin is a token representing digital money), ownership stake (a token can represent a share of a company as tokenized equity), a unique item (NFTs are tokens representing ownership of a specific digital item), a contract (a token can represent the right to something)
- tokenized equity: take a share of Apple stock, issue a digital claim ticket for it on a blockchain. this ticket can be traded at any time, split into tiny fractions, sent anywhere in the world instantly, without relying on a traditional broker or waiting 2 days for settlement.
- short positions:
    - in normal investing, you buy something, hope it goes up, and sell it later for more than you paid.
    - shorting is the opposite: you profit when something goes down in price.
    - bicycle analogy: your neighbor has a bicycle worth $100 today. you think it's going to lose value, so you borrow the bike. you sell it immediately to someone else for $100. you wait for the price to drop, then buy an identical bike for $60. you return this bike to your neighbor and you pocket the $40 difference.
    - shorting stocks are the same concept. when you buy a stock normally, the worst case is it goes to zero and you lose what you paid. your loss is capped. however, when you short, the stock can keep going up forever, so you will lose however much the difference is between how much you bought for and when you sell it.
    - what happened with gamestop?
        - in 2021, people noticed on reddit that gamestop stock was heavily shorted by hedge funds. they all bought the stock together, then driving the price up, and the hedge funds who were short suddenly faced massive losses and were forced to buy shares to cut their losses, which drove the price up even more. this is a vicious cycle called a short squeeze.
