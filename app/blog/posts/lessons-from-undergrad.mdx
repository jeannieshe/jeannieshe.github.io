I’m procrastinating studying for a midterm as I write this, in the quiet tables at my beloved Rotch Library. I tend to let slip to every person I know that I feel like I am reborn every semester of undergrad. You know that feeling when you wake up with only one specific memory from a dream? That’s this, just each semester.

In some ways, college has been so much more soul-defining than I could’ve imagined. I don’t know why I didn’t think I would grow so much. I should’ve known by the way that “young adult” is a whole literary genre. Anyways, a memento for each semester block of time I’ve survived at MIT.

## Semester 1: People you’re meant to meet might not work out. People you’re not meant to meet might work out.
I came into college willing to meet as many people as I could. I thought I could rely on the random roommate assignment process to go smoothly, but sometimes forced relationships don’t result in anything tangible, and things fizzle. At the same time, fleeting moments with people I thought I’d never encounter again can amount to a fulfilling relationship that feels core to my early 20s now.

A word to describe my first semester is regret. I thought about it often, and tried to make careful choices to avoid it in my future. Future self continues to live with no regrets.

## IAP 1: Become friends with the people you work with.
Befriend your bosses. They might become some of the most important mentors that come in clutch when you have future crises. This one’s for you, N, even though every interaction with you feels like I’m in a sitcom.

## Semester 2: Medical insurance changes lives.
Winter break was full of days waking up checking my health portal, checking the progress of my prior authorization of my new medication. My first time trying biologics. February 9th was the first dose, and I learned how to give self-injections and bear the acute pain. J learned to help me too, and my quality of life improved to an undescribable extent. Suddenly, falling asleep within an hour of going to bed could be expected, and even became the norm. Getting through the rest of college without too much pain seemed like a possibility. Moving into a single was the privacy I needed, and I learned how much alone time was important to me mentally.

## Summer 1: Live life on your own schedule. You can survive on an air-fryer, microwave, minifridge, and no stove.
## Semester 3: Take matters into your own hands.
Literally, every step I take is a choice I make. Every day can be distilled into a million small decisions, like which route you end up taking going from place A to place B. So many times this semester, I wanted to bonk my head inwards with the headaches that my own ambition caused me. And yet, believe it or not, I realized that I could cause myself both equally immense stress and relief. Cue pulling up hostelworld.com during 6.1200 lecture; scrawling missed lecture notes in my curtained hostel bed when I finally made it to Paris for iGEM. Beaming, meeting others, sharing our research proudly and loving every moment of having my social energy depleted by people who’s research will define our futures.

## IAP 2: Two introverts, forced into a tiny double, running out of clean clothes with a host balking at their request to run a load of laundry once in three weeks.
Madrid is remembered by more than enough “going out” for my entire life I think. I emerged from a displaced three weeks with one of my closest friends for life.

## Semester 4: Premature tears and a postponed breakup — is it really worth your time?
And my first vacation with a group of friends to date. Miscible memories of Puerto Rico.

## Summer 2: Dare to arrive in a new country, with a new job, no friends, no groceries, and no gym membership. Dare to leave a well visited country, with a job well done, friends well made, a stomach well fed, and weight(s) well lifted.
## Semester 5: The only person holding you back is yourself.
Wear your heart on your sleeve, but for God’s sake, you don’t need to inscribe your thoughts in concrete for others to read. Commit, take risks, it’s hard because it’s new. Switching majors felt like taking a turn without my blinker on three seconds in advance. I wouldn’t change it for the world. Putting myself in difficult classes is worth it because it’s a privilege to learn what I want, to be able to choose and explore and push myself beyond my comfort zone and then some.

## IAP 3: Family trumps academics, always.
## Semester 6: Believe it or not, there are more than two career paths post-Bachelor’s.
For the past years, the crowd I interacted with the most were largely academics. This is, of course, the fault of no one. In the Department of Biological Engineering, it’s hard not to hear the rhetoric of “PhD or bust.” For semesters, I’ve been trying to stomach the idea that I, too, one day will need to apply to grad school — until this semester.

I made the naive decision to take 15.373 Venture Engineering this semester, immediately dismissing it as the definite class I would drop once the workload became too much. Funny how things change, right? I’m in week 6 of the semester and have decided to drop a technical class instead of this entrepreneurship one.

It really caught me off guard to learn that there is a whole field of entrepreneurship that can be weaved into any career you choose to pursue.

I feel rather late to the game that I only just now have sat down and discovered entrepreneurship. There are so many viable career paths post-grad for any student, even if you major in engineering. In 15.373, nearly every Monday we hear from guest speakers. My biggest takeaways have been that what you choose to major in at MIT truly does not determine where you might succeed in the future. No doors are closed after your undergrad; in fact, I would venture to say that MIT produces equally capable individuals no matter their field of study.
